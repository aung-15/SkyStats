Data Critique

Reading the T-100 Dataset Critically

Every dataset is built for a purpose, and that purpose shapes what it counts and what it leaves out. Before drawing conclusions from the T-100 Segment data, we examined where it comes from and what it can't tell us.

Provenance

Where the Data Comes From

The T-100 Segment dataset is part of the Form 41 Traffic Data Bank, managed by the U.S. Bureau of Transportation Statistics (BTS) and funded through the Federal Aviation Administration's (FAA) annual budget. It covers the operational characteristics of the commercial airline industry, split into domestic and international subsections organized by nonstop flight segments.

The underlying records originate with the airlines themselves, who are legally required to report them to BTS — domestic U.S. carriers under 14 CFR Part 241, and foreign carriers with U.S. origin or destination flights under 14 CFR Part 217. That legal mandate is what makes the dataset comprehensive and trustworthy: it isn't voluntary self-reporting, it's regulatory compliance.

A note on flight segments

A single itinerary can become multiple rows. A flight from Buffalo, NY to Los Angeles, CA with a stop in Chicago, IL is recorded as two separate nonstop segments — each one counted as its own "flight."

What's Inside

Variables We Used

The full T-100 dataset is extensive; our project draws on the following fields.

Category Fields
Geography Origin & destination airport code, city, market name, state / FIPS code, world area code
Aircraft & Carrier Unique carrier code & name, aircraft type group / code / configuration, total seats
Operations Departures performed, available seats, non-stop segment passengers transported, distance between airports (miles)
Time Year, month
Classification Service class (we use "F" — passenger), source of data (D = Domestic, I = International)

From these fields, operational metrics like Revenue Passenger Miles (RPMs), Available Seat Miles (ASMs), and load factor can be derived — giving a detailed picture of how full flights are, how busy airports are, and how airline networks change over time. Because the data runs continuously from 1990 through 2026 with no gaps, it's well suited to measuring exactly the kind of before/after trend we needed to study the impact of COVID-19.

The Blind Spots

What the Data Doesn't Show

The T-100 dataset excels at documenting the operational side of air traffic, but it has no humanistic dimension. It doesn't tell us who the passengers were — their age, gender, or socioeconomic status — why they were traveling, or what they paid for their tickets. We can measure that travel collapsed in 2020, but the dataset alone can't tell us what that meant for the people behind the numbers.

It also can't explain why things happened. We can calculate flight delays as the gap between scheduled and actual departure times, but weather conditions and individual aircraft identifiers (tail numbers) aren't included, so the causes of those delays stay invisible. General aviation and military flights are excluded entirely — only commercial air traffic is recorded, even though both can meaningfully affect commercial operations.

Why the gaps exist

T-100 is, at its core, a regulatory and business dataset. It reflects what the government needs to oversee commercial aviation — not what would be most useful to a historian or sociologist. Notably absent: aircraft tail numbers (which would reveal airline business operations) and ticket prices (which would reveal profitability) — both withheld to protect airlines' business confidentiality. That exclusion represents a deliberate balance between regulatory oversight and commercial privacy, not an oversight.

Because of these gaps, we treat T-100 as a strong backbone for measuring what happened to air travel volumes and networks, while relying on the secondary sources listed on our Sources page for the human and behavioral context T-100 can't provide.
