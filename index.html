How COVID-19 Reshaped American Air Travel

In 2020, U.S. commercial air travel collapsed almost overnight. Five years later, national passenger totals look fully recovered — but the recovery was not even. We used federal flight data to trace what actually happened across the ten largest U.S. metropolitan areas.

Research Question

What We're Investigating

How did COVID-19 travel restrictions affect commercial air passenger volume and flight distances across the ten largest U.S. metropolitan areas — and to what extent had these patterns recovered by 2025?

Using the Bureau of Transportation Statistics' T-100 Segment dataset, we tracked monthly passenger counts, departures, and flight distances from 2019 through 2025 for Atlanta, Chicago, Dallas–Fort Worth, Houston, Los Angeles, Miami, New York, Philadelphia, Phoenix, and Washington DC.

The Story Behind the Data

Three Threads We Followed

Beyond the headline collapse, the data revealed uneven, sometimes surprising patterns in how air travel came back.

An Uneven Recovery

National passenger totals surpassed 2019 levels by 2024 — but that average hides a split: six metro areas grew past their pre-pandemic baseline while four, including Los Angeles and Philadelphia, still hadn't caught up by 2025.

See the recovery chart →

Longer Hauls, Fewer Flights

Total departures were still 9.1% below 2019 levels in 2023, even as passenger counts nearly closed the gap — and the average nonstop segment got noticeably longer, hinting at a shift toward fewer, longer routes.

A Dataset Built for Business

The T-100 dataset is comprehensive on operations — seats, routes, carriers — but says nothing about the passengers themselves: who they were, why they flew, or what they paid.
